## Why the Data Matters

Before building any product, a strong PM validates that the problem is big enough to justify the investment. This file documents the scale of the UK regulatory change burden to establish that RegRadar addresses a problem of meaningful economic significance.

---

## Regulatory Publication Volume

### FCA
- The FCA publishes policy statements, consultation papers, guidance consultations, finalised guidance, supervisory statements, and Dear CEO letters across dozens of regulated sectors
- Volume has increased materially since the UK's departure from the EU, as the FCA has taken on responsibility for rules previously set at EU level
- Post-Brexit, the FCA has been running multiple major reform programmes simultaneously, including the Consumer Duty (2023), the Future Regulatory Framework Review, and the Edinburgh Reforms agenda — all generating significant publication volume

### PRA
- The PRA supervises ~1,500 banks, building societies, credit unions, insurers, and major investment firms
- Key ongoing reform programmes include Basel 3.1 implementation (significant capital and liquidity rule changes phased through 2025–2030), operational resilience requirements, and model risk management standards

### Bank of England
- Financial Policy Committee publications, stress testing frameworks, and systemic risk guidance all require triage by affected institutions

### Combined Burden
For a bank subject to FCA, PRA, and Bank of England oversight — which includes all UK-authorised banks — the volume of regulatory publications requiring triage is substantial and non-trivial to manage manually.

## Cost of Compliance

### Industry-Level Data
- Thomson Reuters Cost of Compliance surveys consistently find that senior compliance officers at large financial institutions expect compliance costs to continue rising year on year
- A significant proportion of compliance budgets at large banks are allocated to regulatory change management, including dedicated teams of compliance analysts whose primary function is monitoring and triaging regulatory change
- The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision has noted that the operational burden of regulatory compliance at large internationally active banks has grown substantially over the past decade

### Analyst Time Allocation
- Industry research and interviews with compliance professionals consistently indicate that a significant portion of compliance analyst time — often cited in the range of 40–60% — is spent on reading, summarising, and cross-referencing regulatory documents rather than on higher-value interpretive and advisory work
- This represents a significant opportunity for AI-assisted productivity improvement, given that reading and cross-referencing are precisely the tasks where LLMs with RAG can provide the most direct assistance

## Post-Brexit Regulatory Divergence

One of the most significant drivers of increased regulatory change volume for UK banks is the post-Brexit divergence between UK and EU regulatory frameworks.

Since the UK's departure from the EU's single market, the FCA and PRA have been systematically reviewing inherited EU regulations and deciding which to retain, modify, or replace with UK-specific rules. This process — the FSMA 2023 reform programme — is expected to generate substantial regulatory change over the period 2024–2028.

For UK banks with EU operations, this creates a dual-track compliance challenge: they must simultaneously track UK regulatory change *and* EU regulatory change, increasing the volume of publications requiring triage.

## Regulatory Enforcement Context

The consequences of inadequate regulatory change management are not theoretical:

- The FCA has fined firms for failures attributable in part to inadequate regulatory change management processes
- Consumer Duty, which came into force in 2023, places a positive obligation on firms to demonstrate ongoing compliance — creating a continuous monitoring and assessment requirement rather than a point-in-time compliance exercise
- Operational resilience requirements require firms to demonstrate their ability to stay within impact tolerances for important business services, including compliance functions

The regulatory environment creates both the problem (high volume of change requiring triage) and the incentive for investment (material financial and reputational consequences of getting it wrong).
